¬∑ Reflect on how confident you feel using the problem-solving techniques and process:

Pseudocode

Pseudocode is an informal language that programmers use to create an outline or a rough draft of a program.

I used it to be clear about what needs to be tackled and the methods and steps that will lead me to it.

Trying something

Problem-solving is about trying something out. I constantly try different methods and ways to get the code working. It is a good way to practice and know what works and what doesn‚Äôt. And most importantly why.

Rubber ducky method

The method is referred to as a think-aloud protocol. Programmers explain the problem out loud to get your brain into problem-solving mode. When they talk to a rubber duck, they can get a better idea of their thought processes and gain a deeper understanding of their work.

Reading error messages

The error messages are helpful to spot the problem and show a direction into solving it. I didn‚Äôt notice it until I found how useful it is during the buzz problem.

Console.logging

Console.logging allows programmers to read the code on a web page and therefore help to test the written code.

Googling

You can almost google everything, and get millions of answers. It is important that you know which one will suit the problem you encounter the most. Again, it is not about getting the answer, it‚Äôs to know how and why it resolved the problem.

Asking your peers for help

It is always nice to get support from people who work on the same project as you. They might know the answer to it or have excellent ideas.

Improving your process with reflection

I often ask myself whether I can still solve the same problem without the answer and what are other methods I can use to improve my problem-solving skills. The most important thing is to make sense of the process and apply it to the problems you encounter.
